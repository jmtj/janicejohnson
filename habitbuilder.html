Habit Builder

An app to give health-conscious individuals the ability to manage and improve their health and wellness habits in a centralized location and to enable them to create an accountability network by giving them the ability to share their chosen activities with friends.

I was the only UX Designer for this project that was completed as part of Career Foundry UX Design certification requirement.

Assumptions & Hypothesis

Based on the project objective, I established assumptions that there is a need and desire for users to have a customizable habit tracking application that allows them to create their own accountability network.

I then derived hypotheses of how the app would meet their needs and desires to make users successful in their wellness journeys.

The assumptions and hypotheses drove the direction of my competitive analysis and user research.

Competitive Analysis

The initial competitive analysis was with two apps that offer similar habit tracking options independent of the user’s tracking device, Metriport and HabitShare. Metriport has more features but does not offer sharing with a social network. HabitShare has a social network sharing function, but it is a simpler app with less features and no integrations with tracking devices.

Additional apps were researched throughout the process as new discoveries were made during user research, wireframing, prototyping and testing. These included but not limited to, My Fitness Pal, Habitify, and HabitNow.

SWOT Analysis

Risk/Opportunity

The most prevalent risk is the number of other health and wellness apps available to the target audience. This makes it challenging to acquire market share with all the other noise from other apps and to gain ranking in searches for health and wellness apps. Many users are tied to the apps of their wearable trackable devices, but that limits them to only connections with the same brand of wearable.

There is potential to develop a competitive health and wellness app that

  • does not limit users to the device they wear
  • allows integration with their wearables for auto-load of their fitness activity metrics
  • offers customizable features for metrics and goal setting
  • allows them to connect with their friends to encourage habits for a healthier lifestyle

User Research

User Surveys

Goal of survey:
  • Understand if there is a desire for a single source of wellness and fitness habit tracking
  • Understand if users want the ability to share their progress of their habits with a chosen network to create an accountability network
  • Identify what features are most important to the potential users for tracking their habits and creating an accountability network
Survey results from 113 completed responses indicated:
  • 97% are trying to improve or maintain their wellness behaviors
  • 88% track their habits in some way
  • 78% who track their habits would prefer some sort of centralization
  • 76% believe tracking wellness and fitness behaviors is beneficial to them and 10% were not sure because they have not tried tracking
  • 55% believe sharing with a network is beneficial and 16% were not sure, since they have not tried an accountability partnership

User Interviews

The survey results indicated that there is a need for centralization for tracking and monitoring wellness and fitness behaviors and there is a desire to create a network community to improve their accountability.

The user interviews dove deeper into users’ motivations and to discover what is and is not currently working for them.

Goals of Interviews:
  • understand the motivations of the potential users to use an app for tracking their behaviors
  • discover what is currently working for them and what do they think would be helpful in their wellness journeys
  • explore opportunities to meet the needs of the users to manage their wellness and fitness behaviors

User Research Insights

  • Users are looking for ways to stay-on or get back-on track to create good health and wellness behaviors
    • Tracking is especially helpful when they want to change their behaviors and create new habits
  • Behavior entries must be simple and easy – one click entry or the ability to set a default entry or duplicate existing entries
    • Integration with wearable trackers helps keeps things simple for activity tracking
    • Users don’t want too much noise – if they are not into running, they will not be motivated on information on running
  • Customization is essential to fit everyone's needs and desires
    • Type of behaviors tracked
    • Metrics to measure behaviors
    • Goals to success
    • Reminders and prompts
  • Users are motivated by others’ successes and behaviors, but don’t necessarily want to share ALL their details, therefore users must be able customize what they share – privacy settings are important
  • Some users are motivated with gamification as part of their goal achievements, some like Emoji rating of behavior to log how they are feeling about the behavior or in general
  • Personalized nudges to encourage desired behaviors
  • Reliable and consistent functionality from the app is important – if it is not there, they will stop using the app

Define

Problem Statement

Users want an app to manage their health habits that are important to their individual wellbeing and to get support from their friends who have a similar mindset to build healthier habits.

They want the app to be simple to use without distractions. They want to see their progress and feel accomplished so that they do not lose motivation to keep building better behaviors and habits.

We will know this is true when we see

  • how many users are using the app consistently
  • how many users share the app with their friends
  • how many users are meeting the goals that they set for themselves

User Stories

I broke the proposed features and functionality of the app into user stories to create manageable pieces of work to help ensure that the design focused on the users’ needs and desires.

  • Onboarding
  • Sign-up and Log-in
  • Integrate with major tracking devices
  • Enter activity
  • Share the app

Personas and User Journeys

Design Personas were created to help keep the design process focused on the needs and desires of the users that were established in the research.

These personas give a personification to individuals, by telling their stories of motivations, struggles, and behaviors.

These personas then were used to break down their stories into journey maps of how each persona could experience the app.

Ideate

User Flows

After defining who the users are and theirs needs with personas and journey maps, I created user flows to ensure all the defined functionality, features, and steps, would be developed for the users to complete their tasks.

Some examples:

  • New user logging in for the first time and creating an account.
  • Creating custom behaviors to track and enter the activity for the behavior.

Information Architechture

The user flows were then used to develop the initial site map.

To test the initial version, a digital card sort was created utilizing OptimalSort.

Changes were made to the site map based on the findings from the card sorting activity and prototyping activities.

  • Changed number of main sections from three to four.
  • Updated the names of the main sections to align with card sorting findings.
  • Created additional sub-sections.

Testing

Usability Testing

Starting with mid-fidelity prototypes, usability tests were conducted with 6 participants who met the target audience criteria. The testing provided insight to where the design was lacking intuitiveness, consistency, and unnecessary distractions.

To analyze the results, I used affinity mapping and a rainbow spreadsheet process to group and prioritize the findings.

Sample of updates resulting from Usability Testing

  • Reworked some terminology and added definitions when appropriate.
  • Added coaching tips for some features on first time use by user.
  • Reduced the number of options for “rating” and added example of how “rating” can be used.
  • Increase size of some of the primary CTA buttons.

Iterations

Iterative Improvements

Using Gestalt Laws and Google’s Material Design Guidelines, I created the High-Fidelity prototype ensuring I established hierarchies, emphasis, and groupings of similarities.

I applied grids and margins to ensure good balance and visual organization to my screens.

I applied Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, which resulted in more accessible form fields and better color contrast for the app.

Continuing to Iterate

Next Up

Account management and user preferences have not been fully designed and are critical for the usability and functionality of the application for the users, making them the next priority of design.

The design to this point has focused on fundamental functionality, but during the usability testing and peer collaboration, progress visualizations were identified as desired features to help users see their goal progress.

Visualizations were in the long-term design plan and will be made the next priority in design and protype development after account management and preferences.

Another planned feature of the application to help encourage engagement and accountability, is the ability to connect with users' physical trainers to receive workouts from their trainers and for trainers to see their clients’ progress.

In addition to the standards that have been established for the entire application, these features will have the same usability testing and peer collaboration the existing prototype has undergone to ensure we are creating the desired experience for the users.

Additionally, A/B preference testing will be utilized to get feedback from real potential users of the application.

We will know the design is desirable and the implementation effective when I see how many users are using the tool consistently, how many users share the app with their friends, how many users connect with their physical trainers via the app, and how many users are meeting the goals that they set for themselves.
