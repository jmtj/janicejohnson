Habit Builder

Designed for health-conscious individuals to manage and improve their health and wellness habits and create an accountability network across the different brands of tracking devices that their friends use.

Project Objective

The initial concept is based on the theory that healthy habits are one of the best ways to maintain or improve wellness behaviors but developing or keeping those habits can be challenging for people due to distractions and busy lifestyles.

By providing a centralized and convenient tool that allows people to track their habits and develop a personal accountability network, their chance of improving and maintaining these healthy habits increases.

My Role

I was the only UX Designer for this project that was completed as part of Career Foundry UX Design certification requirement.

My Tools:
  • Figma
  • Crowd Signal
  • Optimal Sort

The Process

Discover

To understand the best focus for my project, I compiled a list of assumptions about what our health-conscious individuals were seeking to help improve their wellness habits and proposed hypotheses that might meet those needs.

I also researched other existing products and conducted user research to understand what is currently available to users and what is and is not working for them.

Based on the project objective, I established assumptions that there is a need and desire for users to have a customizable habit tracking application that allows them to create their own accountability network.

I then derived hypotheses of how the app would meet their needs and desires to make users successful in their wellness journeys.

The assumptions and hypotheses drove the direction of my competitive analysis and user research.

Assumptions Hypothesis
With so many various apps and trackers, users need a centralized place for all their health metrics and progress reporting. Integrate the tool with multiple brands of fitness trackers so users do not have to manually enter their metrics.
Friends do not always use the same brand of trackers which makes it hard to automate social support for their fitness. By enabling integrations of multiple fitness trackers, users are not limited to connecting with their friends on the same fitness app to build a social support system.
Everyone has individual needs: they do not necessarily need to track their last menstrual cycle or calories if those metrics are not important to them. Allow the user to customize which metrics they want to track and remove or hide the ones that are not important to them to give them a personalized experience.
Not all wellness is about physical fitness and users need a way to ensure they are taking care of themselves in other ways too: social connections, meditation, or other self care habits. Allow the user to customize their wellness goals depending on their unique needs; goals might be daily meditation, daily active calorie burn, weekly meeting with friends, or monthly date night with partner.

The initial competitive analysis was with two apps that offer similar habit tracking options independent of the user's tracking device, Metriport and HabitShare.

  • Metriport has numerous features but does not offer sharing with a personal network
  • HabitShare has a personal network sharing function, but it is a simpler app with less features and no integrations with tracking devices

Additional apps were researched throughout the process as new discoveries were made during user research, wireframing, prototyping and testing. These included but not limited to, My Fitness Pal, Habitify, and HabitNow.

Strengths - Weaknesses - Opportunities - Threats



The most prevalent risk is the number of other health and wellness apps available to the target audience. This makes it challenging to acquire market share with all the other noise from other apps and to gain ranking in searches for health and wellness apps. Many users are tied to the apps of their wearable trackable devices, but that limits them to connecting with users with the same brand of wearable.

There is opportunity to develop a competitive health and wellness app that

  • does not limit users to the device they wear
  • allows integration with their wearables for auto-load of their fitness activity metrics
  • offers customizable features for metrics and goal setting
  • allows them to connect with their friends to encourage habits for a healthier lifestyle no matter the brand of wearable they use
User Survey Goals:
  • Understand if there is a desire for a single source of wellness and fitness habit tracking
  • Understand if users want the ability to share their progress of their habits with a chosen network to create an accountability network
  • Identify what features are most important to the potential users for tracking their habits and creating an accountability network
Survey results from 113 completed responses indicated:
  • 97% are trying to improve or maintain their wellness behaviors
  • 88% track their habits in some way
  • 78% who track their habits would prefer some sort of centralization
  • 76% believe tracking wellness and fitness behaviors is beneficial to them and 10% were not sure because they have not tried tracking
  • 55% believe sharing with a network is beneficial and 16% were not sure, since they have not tried an accountability partnership
What they are looking for:

The survey results indicated that there is a need for centralization for tracking and monitoring wellness and fitness behaviors and there is a desire to create a network community to improve their accountability.

The user interviews dove deeper into users' motivations and to discover what is and is not currently working for them.

Goals of Interviews:
  • understand the motivations of the potential users to use an app for tracking their behaviors
  • discover what is currently working for them and what do they think would be helpful in their wellness journeys
  • explore opportunities to meet the needs of the users to manage their wellness and fitness behaviors
  • Users are looking for ways to stay-on or get back-on track to create good health and wellness behaviors
    • Tracking is especially helpful when they want to change their behaviors and create new habits
  • Behavior entries must be simple and easy - one click entry or the ability to set a default entry or duplicate existing entries
    • Integration with wearable trackers helps keeps things simple for activity tracking
    • Users don't want too much noise - if they are not into running, they will not be motivated on information on running
  • Customization is essential to fit everyone's needs and desires
    • Type of behaviors tracked
    • Metrics to measure behaviors
    • Goals to success
    • Reminders and prompts
  • Users are motivated by others' successes and behaviors, but don't necessarily want to share ALL their details, therefore users must be able customize what they share - privacy settings are important
  • Some users are motivated with gamification as part of their goal achievements, some like Emoji rating of behavior to log how they are feeling about the behavior or in general
  • Personalized nudges to encourage desired behaviors
  • Reliable and consistent functionality from the app is important - if it is not there, they will stop using the app

Define

Based on what I discovered in the previous phase, I established definitions of the problems I would solve to ensure I stayed on target during the project. I then defined User Stories, Personas, and User Journeys to initiate the path of my solution.

Users want an app to manage their health habits that are important to their individual wellbeing and to get support from their friends who have a similar mindset to build healthier habits.

They want the app to be simple to use without distractions. They want to see their progress and feel accomplished so that they do not lose motivation to keep building better behaviors and habits.

We will know this is true when we see

  • how many users are using the app consistently
  • how many users share the app with their friends
  • how many users are meeting the goals that they set for themselves

Design Personas were created to help keep the design process focused on the needs and desires of the users that were established in the research.

The personas give a personification to individuals, by telling their stories of motivations, struggles, and behaviors.

These personas then were used to break down their stories into journey maps of how each persona could experience the app.





Ideate

Once the problem statement was defined and I established the target users and their journeys, I mapped out how users might accomplish their tasks with the app. Once I had potential flows, I utilized Optimal Sort with 10 participants to understand how users would group the app’s functionalities. Utilizing those results, I did low fidelity sketches of the screen flows.

After defining who the users are and theirs needs with personas and journey maps, I created user flows to ensure all the defined functionality, features, and steps, would be developed for the users to complete their tasks.

Some examples:

  • New user logging in for the first time and creating an account.
  • Creating custom behaviors to track and enter the activity for the behavior.

The user flows were then used to develop the initial site map.

To test the initial version, a digital card sort was created utilizing OptimalSort.

  • Hybrid Sort: 3 suggested categories and participants had the ability to create their own categories.
  • 10 Participants
  • 13 Cards

Four areas (B-E) of improvement were identified based on the participants responses.

Changes were made to the site map based on the findings from the card sorting activity and prototyping activities.

  • Changed number of main sections from three to four.
  • Updated the names of the main sections to align with card sorting findings.
  • Created additional sub-sections.

Design

Based on the outcomes from ideation process, I created mid-fidelity wireframes to layout the screens then started building out the high-fidelity screens with the app's detail design guidelines and applying standard industry guidelines and principals to ensure a pleasing experience for the users.

Once I was comfortable that the necessary screens were accounted for from my sketches, I used Figma to create mid-fidelity wireframes to protype functions and user flows.

After the wireframes were done, I started laying out the details of the design that would be used throughout the app. I used Figma to house the design guide that would be shared with the developers. Using Figma for the documentation made the iteration process more effective by having the sharable elements in a centralized location as the design developed with iterations.

I applied the Gestalt Laws, Google's Material Design Guidelines and Web Content Accessibility Guidelines, to create the High-Fidelity prototype ensuring I established hierarchies, emphasis, and groupings of similarities.

Emphasis: Color was applied to draw the user's eyes to buttons of content.

Hierarchy: The name of the app and what it is about is at the top of the screen. The “Skip Intro” option was moved to the bottom, putting more focus on what the app is about.

Balance & Alignment : Sections are evenly distributed both horizontally and vertically. Intentional white space was kept to minimize distractions and clutter on the screens.

Law of Similarity: All colors are the chosen shades of green or yellow for a feeling of health and positivity. All CTA buttons use a shade of the green.

Unity and Emphasis: All the primary buttons have a shade of the potential brand color to show they are buttons and to make them pop.

I applied grids and margins to ensure good balance and visual organization to my screens.

Testing

I utilized both peer reviews and user usability testing to get feedback on the design.

Usability testing started early with the mid-fidelity prototypes and continued with the high-fidelity prototypes. Tests were conducted with participants who met the target audience criteria. The testing provided insight to where the design was lacking intuitiveness, consistency, and unnecessary distractions.

To analyze the results, I used affinity mapping and a rainbow spreadsheet process to group and prioritize the findings.

Sample of updates resulting from Usability Testing

  • Reworked some terminology and added definitions when appropriate.
  • Added coaching tips for some features on first time use by user.
  • Reduced the number of options for “rating” and added example of how “rating” can be used.
  • Increase size of some of the primary CTA buttons.
